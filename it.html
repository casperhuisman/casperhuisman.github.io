Mining ancient texts

"In order to be able to outline the development of Pan’s characteristics even further, I have created several scripts enabling
me to compare eighteen texts in which Pan is mentioned. The result is a word cloud for each of these texts in which words close
to the word ‘Pan’ are compared with the other texts for their unicity (appendix 1). To do this, the first of the nine scripts uses
the retrieved texts from the ‘Perseus Digital Library’ website. Within these texts, the word ‘Pan’ has been searched, and the 40
words around ‘Pan’ were isolated. These 40 words are called the ‘context’ of the word ‘Pan’ in that text. Within these ‘contexts’,
all words will be counted, to create a ‘term frequency’ (TF) per ‘context’. Words occurring many times within one ‘context’ will,
therefore, have a higher frequency.

The next step involves ‘weighing’ the words for their importance to a text. To do this, the ‘inverse document frequency’ (IDF)
needs to be calculated. Finally, the ‘TFIDF’ is calculated by multiplying the two, giving each word in each context an own ‘score’.
A higher score means that a term occurs many times within one context, and very little in all the other contexts, therefore, words
with high TFIDF numbers imply a strong relationship with the document in which they occur. While the score-tables (app. 2) list the
fifteen highest scoring words paired with their respective TFIDF scores, the word clouds (app. 1) visualise these scores, with the
words scoring highest being the largest. We can see very clearly, for example, that the word ‘song’ was associated with Pan in the
Homeric hymn to Pan, while it does not occur very prominently in any of the other texts."
